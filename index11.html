The 17th-century Amsterdam canals are a world renowned location of cultural and historical significance with a rich history spanning 400 years of development, expansion, innovation and engineering. The city’s canal ring and its design is an early example of large-scale, coordinated urban planning and forward thinking.

N O O R D H O L L A N D

Amsterdam is located in Noord-Holland, the northwestern province of the Netherlands. It's the capital city and has a population of around 850,000 in the main city area and more than 2 million in the wider metropolitan area, being the largest and most lived-in city in the country. With its famous canal belt and very well preserved 17th century architecture, the city thrives on tourism bringing in millions of visitors each year. Summer from June to August sees the biggest influx of visitors who experience the best of the canals by boat, through a time of late sunsets and lively city atmosphere. It’s also one of the world's most multicultural cities with over 170 nationalities.

Amsterdam has a vast array of canals over 100km in length, around 60 miles. These 165 canals divide the city into a series of small islands connected by over 1200 bridges, with the oldest completed in 1648; the Torensluis. The majority of canals can be accessed by private boats, group tours, or rental boats with many moored on the canal edges next to houseboats. The city’s bridges were built to withstand horse and cart weight of the time, which have since been affected by the trucks and small amount of cars that pass through the canal ring. However, maintenance work is currently underway to strengthen certain canal walls and bridges.

4 H U N D R E D

Four hundred years have gone in to
the creation of the canal ring, known as the Grachtengordel. This term often refers to the grandest four canals; the Herengracht, Keizersgracht, Prinsengracht and the Singel encompassing the old city around Dam Square. Although Amsterdam is older than 400 years, it has gone through multiple expansions leading to the creation of the Grachtengordel, notably the Third Expansion starting in 1613 and later the Fourth Expansion. This long time period, combined with the city sitting on top of millions of wooden piles on swampy ground, gives houses their characteristic lopsided charm.

Since 2010, Amsterdam’s ring of canals were awarded the status of a UNESCO World Heritage site. A recognition for its unique urban development, innovative engineering and incredible water management, it joins famous sites like the Historic Center of Rome. Due to the efforts of the city and private owners, this area and the initial city plan remains very well preserved to this day.

U N E S C O .

Reclaimed

The city of Amsterdam is built entirely on reclaimed swamp land, a characteristic that has defined its shape, purpose and the look of its canal houses. The process of reclaim- ing has lead to many feats of engineer- ing to create a liveable city on water, which in turn generated a prosperous maritime shipping port. As Amsterdam sits below sea level, the work needed to keep the water at bay is a continu- ous one, in combination with new foundations and structural work on the houses. Combined with its greenery and hidden gardens these two forms of nature work in harmony together.

Over hundreds of years and through water management via dams, locks and dykes, Amsterdam residents are able to live several meters below sea level.
Like much of the Netherlands, the city is situated on old, soft banks of peat. The early settlers drained this peat by digging ditches then removing this material. Over time, this series of drainage ditches and compacted land lead to the early form of waterways with higher ground creating livable zones. After reclaiming, the buildings need structural support from piles driven into the sand.

The key importance of Amsterdam’s canals has slowly changed over time.
In the early days, they formed defensive measures surrounding the city like an elaborate moat. Wars in Europe meant the city needed protection from attack, however in the 1600s when the city expanded, the defensive canals were turned into residential areas. As a maritime port, the canals were used to deliver goods from ships in the harbour.

As vehicles began to deliver goods, the purpose changed to social boating and often for tour boats today. To the right shows how many boats would enter central Amsterdam in the 1600s.

Although water in Amsterdam
is a constant source of manag-ement, it did bring immense profits to the early city and business owners. Amsterdam has two key entry points by water through the river Amstel and the IJ. The turning point was the creation of a dam in the center of the old city, known now as Dam Square. This blocked off the water route from the Amstel to the IJ and built a meeting point for hundreds of trading ships. Small boats would bring everything from food, construction materials and flowers to markets every day. In the 1600s during the Dutch Golden Age, Amsterdam was one of the wealthiest and most powerful cities in Europe. The port and its economic influence was a driving factor in the growth.

During the 1800s, the canals started
to provide other uses such as public space and additional areas to live. With a densely populated urban environment and private gardens left for the wealthy upper class, the canals provided an area to socialise. People who worked on barges delivering goods to the city could live on these as well. Over time, the barges remained on the canal sides and many interiors have been renovated into modern homes.

Even though the city is ‘reclaimed’, the incredible houses, churches and buildings
of Amsterdam are sitting on top of millions of wooden piles - very long supports driven down through mud into the sandy layer below. The slow subsidence over time has created the tilted buildings you see today so renewing modern foundations becomes
an absolute necessity.

The wooden piles that support buildings and houses can be around
15-20 meters long. These piles need to pass through the soft ground layer into the sand deeper down to provide enough support. Although the method has worked for hundreds of years, the foundations cannot remain entirely stable over time due to changes in groundwater and subsidence.
Small changes in millimeters cause issues, therefore all buildings have their foundations checked and the piles are renewed with modern reinforced concrete. Piles were first driven into the top layer of sand, however with modern machinery they reach a second layer further down for extra support.

G A R D E N S

For most visitors to Amsterdam, the fact there are large internal private gardens on each canal block is generally a complete secret, as they are entirely hidden from view.

These gardens have always formed an important part to each home. They originate from building regulations, set out when first home owners bought a plot of canal front land. When plots of land were put up for auction in the 1600s, they were more uniform that what we see today. For example, a plot may have been around 190 feet deep from the front and 30 feet wide. Owners were allowed to build their home as high as they wanted within the first 110 feet but there were low height restrictions in the remaining area. This empty zone behind each house would give way to an area perfect for internal gardens and horticulture.

Much of Amsterdam’s charm comes from its buildings, however an equal part comes from it’s greenery, from both trees lining the edges of the canals and the interior gardens
of canal houses. The placing of trees along the canal edges gives the city its distinct look and when first put in place, it was a revolutionary design in contrast to other European cities at the time such as Venice.

C I T Y   L I M I T S

Moving forward a few hundred years to
the 1500s, Amsterdam had become extremely prosperous with the boom of maritime trade
and towards the end of the century, the population almost doubled to 50,000. This position as one
of the wealthiest cities in Europe attracted a high level of people coming to live and work, as well
a tide of those fleeing nearby European wars.
— The city was nearing its limits.

LEFT
‘Plattegrond van Amsterdam’
Balthasar Florisz. van Berckenrode (1625)

During this time the Eighty Years’ War was taking place. A key moment was in 1585 when Antwerp came under control of the Spanish, causing many to flee north to Amsterdam.

1 /   It was of utmost importance to keep Amsterdam safely defended on all sides and if the city needed to expand it had to extend its defensive line first.

2 /   Not all newcomers lived within the city walls but also outside in the open land. This posed defensive issues where enemy may be able to seek refuge.

3 /   Thousands of ships would arrive at Amsterdam every day selling a number of goods at markets inside the city.

‘Plattegrond van Amsterdam, 1500’

T H E   T H I R D .

With defensive concerns and a rapidly increasing population, the city needed to look at plans for expansion. Initially, plans were to push outwards on all sides of the old city, however the council decided that Amsterdam would just expand out to the Western area with the South and East held off for a later time.
This Third Expansion created the Jordaan & the first sections of the famous canals known as the Herengracht, Prinsengracht and Keizersgracht.

LEFT
‘Plattegrond van Amsterdam’
Frans Hogenberg, Cornelis Anthonisz. (1572)

A Significant Time

The timing is significant as 1609 was the start of the Twelve Years Truce, an arrangement with the Spanish and Dutch to stop hostilities with each other. As fighting ceased it allowed Amsterdam to expand outwards without concern of attack.

Creating the Herengracht

The moat that formed the bounds of the old city was widened and named the Herengracht. This would become the most prime real estate in the city, intended for luxurious homes of wealthy merchants. Plots of land were put up for public auction, with many sold quickly within months.

T H E   F O U R T H .

The Third Expansion phase took about 50 years to complete before new work would begin around 1660. Even though the city had created more space for homes and businesses through their planning, this was once again quickly filled inside and outside Amsterdam. Plans were then drawn up for the Fourth Expansion to fill in the southern and eastern and areas to complete the canal ring shape. A harmonious design optimal for transport and defence.

LEFT
‘Map of Amsterdam’
Joan Blaeu (1649) — Biblioteca Nacional de España

Auctions for plots of land allowed wealthy owners to buy even more area, as purchasing a ‘lot’ entitled them to buy the one next to it. This lead to the design and construction of enormous canal front homes, seen in areas such as the Golden Bend on the Herengracht. A number of these buildings are now home to office spaces for international businesses.

Even today, the Canal ring does not make a complete loop around the city, due to a slowing in land sales in the East during the 1600s. This area known as the Plantage was filled with many trees and gardens for socialising. Today, it’s home to ‘Artis’ the Royal Zoo and ‘De Hortus’ Botanical Gardens founded in 1638, one of the oldest in the world.
